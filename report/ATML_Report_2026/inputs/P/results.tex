\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:results-P}

We organise the experiments around three blocks: (i) the Table~2a linear-probe and Table~2b zero-shot reproductions, (ii) the Figure~8 ablation in $N$ register tokens, and (iii) an additional independent variant of the test-time register method designed to test whether the failure of \citet{jiang2025vision} at large $N$ is caused by broadcast-node duplication. Numbers in absolute terms cannot match the original paper because of the substantially smaller subsets used for both training and evaluation; the comparison we draw is on the \emph{relative} effect of adding registers.

\subsubsection{Results reproducing original paper}

The DINOv2 row is the cleanest comparison: both checkpoints come directly from \citet{darcet2024vision}, and the trained-register variant improves on all three metrics, replicating the central claim of the paper. The DeiT-III row tests whether registers can simply be ``bolted on'' to a model that has never seen them during training: as expected, this fails on Top-1 (-0.67), but interestingly the patch-level features still improve mIoU and RMSE.

\subsubsection{Results beyond original paper}

\subsubsubsection{Additional Result 1 -- Test-time registers on DINOv2 (re-implementation of Jiang et al.)}

We re-implemented \citet{jiang2025vision} Algorithm~1 (\textsc{FindRegisterNeurons}) and the test-time intervention in PyTorch, on top of the \texttt{timm} DINOv2 baseline. Algorithm~1 was run on 100 ImageNet training images: it identified 50 register neurons concentrated in the upper MLP blocks (17 in block~17, 9 each in blocks 14 and 15, 7 in block~16, the remaining 8 spread across blocks 0--13), with the top neuron (block~17, neuron~884) scoring $151.6$ -- nine times the second-highest. Five of the top-10 neurons matched the indices published by \citet{jiang2025vision} for the Meta-DINOv2 SwiGLU architecture, despite the architectural difference -- this is a strong independent validation that the algorithm finds a stable set.

We then evaluated DINOv2 with $N=4$ test-time registers on the same three Table~2a probes, plus the Figure~8 sweep. Numerical results are reported in Tables~\ref{tab:t2a-jiang} and~\ref{tab:f8-jiang}; the corresponding qualitative attention maps are in Figure~\ref{fig:f8-top-jiang}.

The drop is not predicted by the original paper, which evaluates $N=1$ only (and tests $N=2,3$ in appendix~A.5 without observing this effect).

\subsubsubsection{Additional Result 2 -- ``Independent'' variant: distributing top-$N$ outliers across $N$ registers}

Inspecting Jiang's reference code (\texttt{shared/hook\_fn.py}, function \texttt{activate\_on\_registers}) we noticed that for each register neuron the top-1 outlier value is duplicated identically across all $N$ test-time registers. We hypothesised that the collapse at $N=16$ above might be caused by this duplication: the CLS token sees 16 near-identical broadcast nodes and dilutes its global-readout attention across them. To test this we introduced a variant in which, for each register neuron, the top-$N$ outlier patches are picked and their signed values are written onto the $N$ different test-time registers (so TT\_REG\_0 receives the top-1, TT\_REG\_1 the top-2, and so on). The remainder of the intervention -- zeroing every patch position on those neurons -- is identical to Jiang's. Numerical results are in Table~\ref{tab:f8-jiang-indep}; qualitative maps in Figure~\ref{fig:f8-top-jiang-indep}; the bottom panel in Figure~\ref{fig:f8-bot-jiang-indep}.

The hypothesis is falsified. Distributing $N$ distinct outliers across $N$ separate test-time registers yields essentially the same Top-1 profile as duplicating the top-1 outlier $N$ times: identical plateau at 95.33\% from $N=1$ to $N=8$, identical $\sim$3-point drop at $N=16$. Diagnostic measurements of the patch-token norms after the forward pass confirm that the patches themselves are clean at all $N\geq 1$ (max patch norm $\sim$51, no patch above 60), so the collapse is not a failure of the neuron-zeroing step. The fact that the failure mode is invariant under this controlled change of the writing policy suggests the bottleneck is downstream: 16 strongly active broadcast tokens disrupt the self-attention in the six MLP layers (blocks 18--23) that follow the intervention point and that were never trained to see such input.
