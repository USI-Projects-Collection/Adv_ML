The DINOv2 row reproduces the central claim: trained registers improve all three probes (+0.67~pt Top-1, +0.49~pt mIoU, $-0.775$~m RMSE). Although the gains on classification are modest, the RMSE improvement on NYUd is a $39\%$ relative reduction, which is the kind of dense-prediction effect the paper emphasises. OpenCLIP with test-time registers shows no Top-1 change and small dense-prediction gains, consistent with \citet{jiang2025vision}. The DeiT-III result is the most interesting: injecting four \emph{untrained} register-token parameters at inference into a model that never saw them during pre-training produces a small $-0.67$~pt Top-1 regression but still improves mIoU ($+0.38$~pt) and RMSE ($-0.023$~m). This suggests that even random register slots act as a partial sink for high-norm outliers, supporting the paper's mechanistic interpretation that the registers' role is primarily to absorb artifact tokens rather than to encode dedicated information. The $-0.67$~pt zero-shot drop in Table~2b corresponds to one misclassified image on the 150-image val set and is within subset variance, consistent with the conclusion of \citet{jiang2025vision} that test-time registers do not significantly degrade zero-shot performance.

\subsubsection{Figure 8 -- Attention maps and linear evaluation vs.\ number of registers}

The original Figure~8 of \citet{darcet2024vision} sweeps a model trained from scratch with $N \in \{0,1,2,4,8,16\}$ register tokens. Without retraining we cannot reproduce this exactly, so we compare three approximations: (1)~truncating or cycling the registers of the released DINOv2-reg4 checkpoint, (2)~the test-time register intervention of \citet{jiang2025vision} on the no-register DINOv2 baseline, and (3)~our independent variant of the latter. Each approximation tests a different aspect: (1)~probes how robust an already-trained register model is to changes in $N$ at inference; (2)~tests whether a register-free model can be retrofitted with the register \emph{behaviour} at inference; (3)~controls for the value-duplication design choice in Jiang's intervention. Figure~\ref{fig:f8-top} shows the last-layer CLS$\to$patch attention maps; Figure~\ref{fig:f8-bot} shows the corresponding downstream metrics, with numerical values in Table~\ref{tab:f8}.

Three observations stand out. \textbf{First}, both test-time register methods confirm the paper's core claim qualitatively and quantitatively at $N=1$: a single register is sufficient to absorb the high-norm outlier (the attention map cleans up immediately) and Top-1 jumps from $94.00\%$ to $95.33\%$ -- a gain consistent in sign with the $+0.5$~pt the paper reports for trained registers. \textbf{Second}, both methods plateau at the same $95.33\%$ from $N=1$ all the way through $N=8$, replicating the flat shape of the paper's Figure~8 bottom-left panel. The depth (RMSE) and segmentation (mIoU) panels are noisier because the linear heads for those tasks have far more parameters than the logistic regression head and overfit more readily on our 350-image training subset. \textbf{Third}, both methods exhibit a sharp Top-1 collapse at $N=16$ ($-3.3$~pt for Jiang, $-2.7$~pt for the independent variant). This is the most informative finding beyond the paper: since the independent variant rules out value duplication as the cause, and diagnostic measurements confirm that patch-token norms after the intervention are clean at all $N\geq1$ ($\max \approx 51$, no patch above $60$), the collapse cannot be attributed to residual artifacts in the patch stream. Instead it points to a disruption in the six post-intervention transformer blocks (18--23) that were never trained on so many strongly active broadcast tokens. The test-time intervention is therefore correctly scoped at $N=1$ but does not scale to the very large $N$ values the paper sweeps through with retraining.
