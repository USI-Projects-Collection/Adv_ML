\subsection{Discussion}

Our experiments qualitatively support the central claim of \citet{darcet2024vision}: register tokens do not degrade downstream linear evaluation and modestly improve it. The DINOv2 row of Table~\ref{tab:t2ab} -- the only fully apples-to-apples comparison -- shows gains on all three probes, with a large $39\%$ relative reduction in NYUd RMSE consistent with the paper's emphasis on dense-prediction tasks. The OpenCLIP and DeiT-III rows, which use approximate substitutes for the unreleased trained-register checkpoints, are consistent in sign and small in magnitude. Figure~\ref{fig:f8-bot} reproduces the qualitative shape of the paper's Figure~8 bottom panel for both test-time methods: a sharp jump at $N=1$ and a plateau. The most informative finding beyond the paper is the $N=16$ Top-1 collapse, which our independent variant proves is not caused by value duplication but by downstream blocks (18--23) unable to process many simultaneously active broadcast tokens.

\subsubsection{What was easy}
The DINOv2 portion of Table~2a was the easiest, because both the baseline and the 4-register checkpoint are released directly by the authors via \texttt{timm}; the comparison is fully apples-to-apples with no architectural ambiguity. The three linear probes are also straightforward: each is a single \texttt{Conv2d 1x1} or a scikit-learn logistic regression on top of frozen features, well within an MPS-only compute budget. Re-implementing \citet{jiang2025vision} Algorithm~1 from scratch was easier than expected: the algorithm is short and unambiguous, and the fact that five of the top-10 register neurons we identified on the \texttt{timm} GELU port matched the indices the authors published for the Meta-DINOv2 SwiGLU port gave us immediate, architecture-independent confidence that the re-implementation was correct.

\subsubsection{What was difficult}
The hardest part was building credible with-register baselines for OpenCLIP and DeiT-III: \citet{darcet2024vision} report trained-register results for both, but neither checkpoint has been released publicly. We substituted the OpenCLIP trained variant with the test-time register checkpoint of \citet{jiang2025vision} and built a DeiT-III ``with registers'' by injecting untrained parameter tokens at inference. Both substitutions are documented limitations rather than faithful reproductions. A second difficulty concerned the visualisation of Figure~8 (top): at $N=0$ \citet{darcet2024vision} show several distinct outlier hotspots, while ours shows one dominant outlier. After exhaustive diagnostics (verifying model weights, preprocessing, and the underlying patch-norm distribution), we concluded that the discrepancy reflects a visualisation choice rather than a model-level difference. A third difficulty was a non-obvious bug in our independent variant: zeroing only the top-$N$ outlier patch positions (instead of all $\sim 1369$ patch positions) left residual outliers that re-polluted the attention map. The bug was visible only by measuring patch norms after the forward hook; once fixed, the variant produced clean maps but, importantly, did not rescue the $N=16$ collapse -- a null result that is itself informative.
